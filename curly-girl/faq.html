Troubleshooting
Why is my hair STILL frizzy after starting CGM?+

Frizz has multiple causes. The most common for CGM beginners:

  • Apply styling products to soaking wet hair (not damp, dripping wet)
  • Use more gel than you think. If you think you've used enough, use more
  • Don't touch your hair while it dries. Let the gel cast form, then scrunch out only when 100% dry
  • Sleep on a silk/satin pillowcase or pineapple (loose high ponytail)
  • If in a hard water area (most of England), mineral buildup causes frizz. Use a chelating shampoo monthly
UK products: Boots Essentials Gel (99p), VO5 Mega Hold Gel (~£2-5), Garnier Banana Hair Food (~£4)
My curls fall flat by day 2. How do I make them last?+

Nighttime: Pineapple (gather loosely at crown with silk scrunchie), satin bonnet, or satin pillowcase.

Day 2 refresh:

  1. Shake hair out gently for volume
  2. Mist flat/frizzy sections with water spray bottle
  3. Scrunch in a tiny amount of gel or leave-in
  4. Diffuse briefly on low heat, or air dry

For fine/wavy hair: less water is more. Over-wetting flattens waves.

Budget: Primark satin pillowcase (~£4-6) | Specialist: Only Curls Satin Pillowcase (~£20)
My hair feels like straw. What am I doing wrong?+

Usually means protein overload or severe dryness. Do the strand test:

  • Snaps immediately = protein overload (too much protein, not enough moisture)
  • Stretches endlessly then breaks = moisture overload
  • Stretches slightly then springs back = balanced

If protein overload: Use a protein-free deep conditioner. Avoid products with "hydrolyzed" proteins for a few weeks.

If severe dryness: Deep condition weekly, use a leave-in, seal with oil.

Moisture: Garnier Hair Food Banana Mask (~£4-6) | Protein: Shea Moisture JBCO Treatment Masque (~£10)
Products make my hair look greasy and weighed down.+

Common causes:

  • Too much product: Start with a tiny amount and add more only if needed
  • Products too heavy: Wavies and fine-haired people need lightweight gels/mousses, not creams and butters
  • Not clarifying enough: Use a clarifying shampoo every 2-4 weeks
  • Co-washing when you need shampoo: Not everyone suits co-washing. Try a gentle sulphate-free shampoo instead
Lightweight gel: Boots Essentials Gel (99p) | Mousse: Wella Shockwaves Volume Mousse (~£3)
My scalp is itchy since starting CGM.+

Your scalp may need more cleansing than CGM recommends. Scalp health comes first.

  • Clarify more often: every 1-2 weeks instead of monthly
  • Use a medicated shampoo if needed: Head & Shoulders, Nizoral, T/Gel. Apply to scalp only, protect lengths with conditioner
  • Try a scalp scrub or scalp brush during washing
  • If you switched to co-washing, go back to a gentle shampoo
Important: Persistent itching, flaking, or redness may need a GP or dermatologist. Don't self-diagnose seborrheic dermatitis.
I've been doing CGM for weeks and it's not working.+

CGM takes 4-8 weeks minimum. Timeline:

  • Week 1-2: Adjustment period, may look worse before better
  • Week 3-4: Scalp starts adjusting, first signs of curl pattern
  • Month 2-3: Noticeable improvement in definition and health
  • Month 6+: If heat-damaged, new undamaged growth starts making visible difference

If still no improvement after 8 weeks, review: Are you applying to soaking wet hair? Using enough gel? Not touching while drying? Clarifying regularly?

Technique
What is "squish to condish" and how do I do it?+

A technique for getting conditioner deep into your hair:

  1. Apply conditioner in the shower
  2. Cup a handful of water and press it into your hair
  3. You should hear a squelching sound as water pushes conditioner in
  4. Repeat until hair feels slippery and conditioned
  5. Don't rinse fully (leave some conditioner in)

Best for: Medium to high porosity hair. Low porosity hair may find this causes buildup.

How do I plop my hair?+

Plopping uses a T-shirt or microfibre towel to set curls while removing excess water:

  1. Lay a T-shirt flat on a surface
  2. Flip your head forward and lower your curls onto the centre
  3. Wrap the edges around your head and tie at the back
  4. Leave for 10-30 minutes (not overnight for most people)
Wavies: Plopping may flatten your waves. Try micro-plopping instead: scrunch upward briefly with a microfibre towel, then let hair hang free to dry.
How do I diffuse properly?+
  • Use low heat, medium speed
  • Flip head upside down for volume
  • Cup sections of hair in the diffuser bowl, bring to your head, hold for 20-30 seconds
  • Don't move the diffuser around (causes frizz)
  • Alternate sides to dry evenly
  • Stop when 80% dry, let the rest air dry

For wavies: Diffusing is often essential. Air drying causes water weight to pull waves straight.

What is "scrunching out the crunch" (SOTC)?+

When gel dries, it forms a hard "cast" around your curls. This cast preserves definition while drying. Once 100% dry, scrunch your hair gently to break the cast. The crunchiness disappears, leaving soft, defined curls.

  • Wait until completely dry: touching too early = frizz
  • Add a tiny drop of oil to your palms before scrunching for extra shine
  • Scrunch from the bottom up
Best SOTC gels (UK): Boots Essentials Gel (99p), Eco Styler Olive Oil (~£3), Giovanni LA Hold (~£6-10, glycerin-free)
What's a basic wash day routine for beginners?+
  1. Cleanse: Gentle sulphate-free shampoo on scalp only
  2. Condition: Apply conditioner mid-lengths to ends. Detangle with wide-tooth comb or fingers. Squish to condish. Rinse mostly out (leave a little)
  3. Style: On soaking wet hair, scrunch in gel. Flip upside down and scrunch from ends up
  4. Dry: Plop in T-shirt for 10-20 min, then air dry or diffuse
  5. SOTC: When 100% dry, scrunch out the gel cast

UK Budget Starter Kit (under £5)

  • Shampoo: Aldi Lacura (~£1.29)
  • Conditioner: Tesco/Superdrug own brand (~£1)
  • Gel: Boots Essentials Gel (99p)
  • Towel: Old cotton T-shirt (free)
Product Questions
How do I check if a product is CGM approved?+

Use our Ingredient Checker: paste the full ingredient list and it flags any issues.

Quick rules:

  • Avoid ingredients ending in "-cone", "-conol", "-xane" (silicones) unless preceded by "PEG-" (water-soluble)
  • Avoid sodium lauryl/laureth sulfate, ammonium lauryl/laureth sulfate
  • OK: Fatty alcohols (cetyl, cetearyl, stearyl) are moisturising, NOT drying
  • OK: Behentrimonium methosulfate is NOT a sulphate despite the name

Do I need a "final wash" before starting CGM?+

Yes. One wash with a sulphate shampoo strips away silicone and product buildup. This gives CGM-friendly products a clean slate. After this single wash, switch to your sulphate-free routine.

Any cheap sulphate shampoo works: VO5, Pantene, Alberto Balsam. You likely already have one at home.

What's a clarifying shampoo vs a chelating shampoo?+
  • Clarifying shampoo uses surfactants to remove product buildup, oils, and silicones
  • Chelating shampoo contains chelating agents (EDTA, phytic acid) that specifically bind to and remove mineral deposits from hard water

If you're in a hard water area, a regular clarifying shampoo won't fully remove mineral buildup. You need a chelating shampoo for that.

Chelating: Malibu C Hard Water Shampoo (~£15, Amazon UK) | Budget: Apple cider vinegar rinse (1 part ACV, 3 parts water)
What are "good" vs "bad" alcohols in hair products?+

Good alcohols (moisturising): Cetyl alcohol, cetearyl alcohol, stearyl alcohol, lauryl alcohol, myristyl alcohol. Derived from natural fats, help condition hair.

Bad alcohols (drying): Isopropyl alcohol, ethanol, SD alcohol, alcohol denat, propanol. Strip moisture and cause dryness/frizz.

Use our Ingredient Checker to instantly flag any drying alcohols.

I keep buying products that don't work. How do I stop wasting money?+

Before buying anything, determine:

  1. Porosity: Does hair absorb water quickly or resist it?
  2. Thickness: Individual strand: fine, medium, or coarse?
  3. Density: How many strands overall?

Then match: Fine + low porosity = lightweight, water-based products. Coarse + high porosity = richer products, oils to seal.

Money-saving tip: Buy travel/mini sizes first. Superdrug and Boots stock minis of many ranges (~£2-3).

UK-Specific
I'm in a hard water area and my hair is awful. What can I do?+

Hard water deposits calcium and magnesium on your hair, blocking moisture absorption.

Solutions (cheapest first):

  1. Apple cider vinegar rinse (~£2): 1 part ACV to 3-4 parts water, pour over hair after shampooing. Monthly
  2. Chelating shampoo (~£7-15): Every 2-4 weeks. Look for EDTA or phytic acid
  3. Shower filter (~£20-40): Reduces chlorine but doesn't remove calcium/magnesium fully
  4. Shower head water softener (~£30-60): Ion-exchange cartridges, needs regular replacement

Most of London, South East, East Anglia, and the Midlands have hard to very hard water. Scotland, Wales, and North West tend to be soft.

Wavy Hair (Type 2)
Is CGM different for wavy hair vs curly hair?+

Yes. Wavy hair needs significant CGM modifications:

  • Co-washing usually doesn't work. Use a gentle sulphate-free shampoo instead
  • Clarify more often (every 1-2 weeks, not monthly)
  • Lightweight products only. Gels and mousses, not creams and butters
  • Less product overall. A 10p-coin amount where a curly uses a palmful
  • Plopping may flatten waves. Try micro-plopping instead
  • Diffusing often essential. Air drying can cause waves to drop out
Products feel too heavy on my wavy hair. What should I use?+

Avoid anything marketed as "deeply moisturising", "for coils", or with shea butter/coconut oil high in ingredients.

Lightweight picks for UK wavies:
Mousse: Wella Shockwaves Volume Mousse (~£3)
Gel: Boots Essentials Gel (99p)
Conditioner: Aldi Lacura (~£1.29), rinse thoroughly
Skip leave-ins, or use a tiny spray leave-in only
Should wavies co-wash?+

Most wavies do better with a gentle shampoo rather than co-washing. Co-washing can leave wavy hair limp, greasy, and lacking volume because wavy hair is typically finer and produces more oil.

If you want to try: limit to once a week max, alternate with a low-poo. If hair feels heavy, it's not for you.

My waves only last a few hours then go straight.+

Before giving up:

  • Reset wash (sulphate shampoo) to remove all buildup
  • Use only gel (no cream, no leave-in) on soaking wet hair
  • Scrunch thoroughly (not just a casual squeeze)
  • Diffuse rather than air dry (water weight pulls waves out)
  • Less conditioner, rinsed more thoroughly

If your hair waves when wet but dries straight, you likely do have waves. They just need the right support.

What's the difference between 2A, 2B, and 2C hair?+
  • 2A: Slight S-shaped bend, usually fine and thin, minimal volume. Very easily weighed down
  • 2B: More defined S-waves from mid-length, some frizz, medium texture
  • 2C: Well-defined waves from the root, can form loose ringlets at ends, thicker, more frizz-prone

2A needs lightest products and most frequent clarifying. 2C can handle slightly heavier products. Take our Hair Quiz to find your type.

Beginner Mistakes
I started CGM and my hair is oilier than ever.+

This is the "transition period". Your scalp was producing extra oil to compensate for harsh sulphates stripping it daily. It takes 2-6 weeks to adjust.

  • Wash every 2-3 days with your gentle cleanser during this period
  • Focus shampoo on scalp only, conditioner on lengths only
  • Clarify if it becomes unbearable (not "cheating", it's good hair care)
  • Using a low-poo shortens the transition vs going co-wash only
I bought the same products as someone on Reddit but my hair looks nothing like theirs.+

Hair varies enormously. Products are only one piece of the puzzle. What matters more:

  • Applying to soaking wet hair
  • How much product you use (varies hugely by hair type)
  • Drying method and technique
  • Your water type (hard vs soft)
  • Your hair's porosity
  • Consistency over weeks, not days
I tried not brushing my hair and it's a matted mess.+

CGM says don't brush dry curly hair (breaks up curl clumps, causes frizz). You should still detangle!

Detangle in the shower with conditioner in, using a wide-tooth comb or fingers, working from ends upward.

For wavies: Some wavies find brushing dry before a shower helps. This is fine. Do what works for you.

Do I need to throw away all my old products?+

No. Keep one sulphate shampoo for clarifying (you'll need it every few weeks). Start with just a CGM-friendly conditioner and one gel. Replace other products gradually as they run out.

Do I really need all these products?+

No. A functional routine needs three products: cleanser, conditioner, one styler. Everything else is optional.

Hair Science
What is hair porosity and why does it matter?+

Porosity is how easily your hair absorbs and retains moisture. More important than curl type for choosing products.

  • Low porosity: Cuticle tightly closed. Water beads up. Products sit on top. Takes ages to dry. Use lightweight, water-based products with warmth
  • Medium porosity: Balanced. Most products work. Easiest to maintain
  • High porosity: Cuticle open/damaged. Absorbs water instantly, loses it fast. Needs heavier products, oils to seal, protein treatments

Quick test: Spray water on clean, product-free hair. Beads up (low), soaks in within seconds (medium), absorbs instantly (high).

What is the protein-moisture balance?+

Healthy hair needs both protein (strength) and moisture (flexibility). Too much of either is a problem.

Protein overload: Hair feels rough, stiff, straw-like, brittle. Fix: protein-free deep conditioner, avoid "hydrolyzed" proteins for a few weeks.

Moisture overload: Hair feels mushy, limp, overly soft, curls won't hold shape. Fix: clarify, reduce heavy conditioners, add a protein treatment.

Most people need protein every 2-4 weeks, not every wash. Fine/high-porosity hair generally needs more protein.

What are humectants and why do they matter for UK weather?+

Humectants (like glycerin) attract water from the environment into your hair.

  • Moderate humidity (most UK weather): Humectants work well
  • Muggy summers: Too much moisture pulled in = frizz. Switch to glycerin-free products
  • Winter cold snaps: Humectants pull moisture OUT of your hair = dryness

UK weather is moderate enough that glycerin works fine most of the year. Adjust only in extremes.

Glycerin-free gels: Giovanni LA Hold (~£6-10, TK Maxx)
Are silicones actually bad for curly hair?+

It's complicated.

  • Non-water-soluble silicones (dimethicone, amodimethicone) coat hair and only come off with sulphates = buildup cycle
  • Water-soluble silicones (PEG-modified) wash out with gentle cleansers and can help with frizz

Nuanced view: If you occasionally clarify with sulphates, using silicones is fine. Some high-porosity hair benefits from silicones for sealing the cuticle. Strict avoidance isn't necessary for everyone.

Does the curl type system actually matter?+

It helps with general communication but it's one of the least useful things for choosing products.

More useful properties:

  1. Porosity (how hair absorbs moisture) = determines ingredients
  2. Width/thickness (strand diameter) = determines product weight
  3. Density (total number of strands) = determines product amount
  4. Elasticity (protein-moisture balance) = determines treatments

Curl type tells you what your curls look like, not what they need.

Why does my hair behave differently in different seasons?+
  • Winter: Central heating dries indoor air. May need more moisture. Consider a humidifier
  • Spring/Autumn: Usually the best curl weather in the UK (moderate humidity)
  • Summer: Higher humidity can cause frizz or better definition (depends on porosity)
  • Rain: UK rain is soft water, which can actually be good for curls

Adjust your routine seasonally rather than using the same products year-round.

More Common Questions
Can I still colour my hair on CGM?+

Yes. Chemical processing raises the cuticle and increases porosity, so colour-treated hair needs more moisture and protein. Semi-permanent is gentler than bleach.

After colouring: deep condition, increase protein treatments, be gentler with manipulation, expect to need slightly heavier products.

How often should I wash my curly/wavy hair?+
  • Wavy (2A-2C): Every 2-3 days
  • Curly (3A-3C): Every 3-5 days
  • Coily (4A-4C): Every 5-10 days

Listen to your scalp and hair. There's no prize for going the longest between washes.

Do I need a special haircut for curly hair?+

A good cut makes a huge difference. Many prefer a "dry cut" where the stylist cuts each curl in its natural shape.

UK specialists: Search Curly Hair Artistry directory or Only Curls salon locator. Expect £50-100+ for specialist cuts.

Budget: Ask any hairdresser to cut your hair dry and in its natural state rather than wet and combed straight.

What's a "pineapple" and how do I do it?+

A sleeping technique to preserve curls overnight:

  1. Flip head forward
  2. Gather all hair at the very top of your head
  3. Secure loosely with a silk/satin scrunchie

Key word: loosely. A tight ponytail creates dents.

If hair too short: Try "medusa clipping" (multiple small claw clips at the crown) or a satin bonnet/pillowcase.

My hair looks great wet but terrible dry.+

Very common. Usually means:

  • Not enough hold product: Gel creates a cast that preserves wet-look definition
  • Air drying pulls curls out: Diffuse or plop instead
  • Products too heavy: Curls elongate and lose definition as they dry
  • High porosity: Cuticle opens as it dries, causing frizz. Seal with oil or gel

Fix: More gel, applied to wetter hair, dried with a diffuser.

Can I use a regular towel to dry my hair?+

Regular terry cloth towels cause friction, frizz, and break up curl clumps. Switch to:

  • Microfibre towel (Primark £3-4, Boots £5-8)
  • Old cotton T-shirt (free, most people have one)

Scrunch gently upward. Never rub.

My hair has different curl patterns in different sections. Is that normal?+

Completely normal. Most people have 2-3 different curl patterns on their head. Crown, nape, sides, and front often behave differently due to sun exposure, sleeping position, and natural variation.

Treat different sections differently if needed (e.g., more gel on the frizzy canopy, lighter products on the defined underlayer).

Is CGM actually backed by science?+

Parts are, parts aren't.

Science-backed: Sulphates can strip natural oils. Non-water-soluble silicones cause buildup. Heat damage is cumulative. Friction disrupts curl clumps.

Not well-supported: "Silicones are always bad." "Never use sulphates." "Co-washing cleans effectively" (many dermatologists disagree).

CGM is best used as a starting framework, then adapted based on what your hair actually responds to.

I have dandruff. Can I still do CGM?+

Scalp health comes first, always. If you need medicated shampoo (Head & Shoulders, Nizoral, T/Gel), use it.

Compromise: Apply CGM conditioner to lengths before washing (protects from sulphates), use medicated shampoo on scalp only, then style as normal.

What's the "bowl method"?+

A technique using a bowl of water to encourage curl clumps during styling:

  1. After conditioning, apply styler to soaking wet hair
  2. Fill a bowl with water
  3. Dip hair sections into the bowl and scrunch upward
  4. The excess water distributes product and encourages clumping

Best for: People wanting maximum definition. May be too much water for fine/wavy hair.
